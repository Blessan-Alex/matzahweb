## The Core Sadhya Menu: Where Tradition Meets Celebration

If you are having a Kerala wedding, the Sadhya is the heart of it. There is really no way around it. Even couples who want a modern, multi cuisine spread almost always include a Sadhya component because it connects the celebration to something deeper. It is cultural identity served on a banana leaf.

A complete wedding Sadhya typically includes 24 to 28 dishes, each placed at a specific spot on the leaf. This is not random. There is a logic to how the meal unfolds, and experienced caterers know the sequence by heart.

Here is what a proper wedding Sadhya menu looks like:

### The Foundation

- **Choru (Rice):** Kerala matta rice or white rice, served in two rounds. The foundation everything else builds on.
- **Ghee:** A small pool of pure ghee on the rice. This is where the meal begins. Ghee, rice, and parippu. Simple. Perfect.
- **Parippu Curry:** Toor dal cooked with coconut and tempered with mustard seeds. The first thing guests eat.

### The Curries and Sides

- **Sambar:** Thick, rich, loaded with drumstick and vegetables. Wedding sambar should have depth, not just heat.
- **Rasam:** Peppery, tangy, served in the second round with rice. Cuts through the richness of everything else.
- **Avial:** Mixed vegetables in coconut and yogurt. This is the signature dish of any Sadhya. If the avial is good, people trust the rest.
- **Thoran:** Finely chopped cabbage or beans with grated coconut. Dry, crunchy, and essential for texture contrast.
- **Olan:** Ash gourd and cowpeas in thin coconut milk. Gentle and subtle. The quiet one on the leaf that you notice only when it is missing.
- **Koottukari:** Black chickpeas and raw banana in coconut gravy. Mildly sweet, deeply satisfying.
- **Kurukku Kaalan:** Yam and raw banana in thick yogurt and coconut gravy. Creamy, cooling, and one of the dishes that separates a good Sadhya from a great one.
- **Pulissery:** Ripe mango or ash gourd in a sour yogurt gravy. A perfect palate cleanser.
- **Pachadi:** Pineapple or cucumber in seasoned yogurt. Cool, sweet, tangy all at once.
- **Mezhukkupuratti:** Banana stir fry with minimal spices. Simple, delicious, and often underestimated.
- **Erissery:** Pumpkin and lentil cooked in roasted coconut paste. Earthy, grounding, wonderful.

### The Accompaniments

- **Pappadam:** Crispy, fresh, never stale. You need at least four per guest because people keep reaching for more.
- **Banana chips (Upperi):** Fried in coconut oil. Crunchy and slightly salty.
- **Pickles:** Mango pickle and lime pickle. Both should have real heat and tang.
- **Banana:** Ripe nendran banana. Eaten toward the end of the meal.
- **Buttermilk (Sambaram):** Served last. Spiced, cool, and the perfect finish.

### The Grand Finale

- **Payasam:** Minimum two varieties. Palada pradhaman is the traditional star. Parippu pradhaman (moong dal based) is the second most popular. Semiya payasam works beautifully as a third option. For weddings with bigger budgets, some families add a fourth: pal payasam or adapradhaman.

### Popular Add Ons for a Kerala Wedding Sadhya

Many families now include non vegetarian dishes alongside the traditional vegetarian Sadhya. These are usually served separately, either as a side or at a dedicated counter:

- **Fish curry** (meen curry with raw mango or kokum)
- **Prawn masala** or **prawn theeyal**
- **Chicken roast** (Kerala style with thick masala)
- **Kappa biryani** (tapioca based, a Central Kerala specialty that has become incredibly popular)
- **Duck roast** (for those wanting something extra special)

These add ons do not replace the Sadhya. They complement it. And they give non vegetarian guests something to get excited about alongside the traditional spread.

## Multi Cuisine Options: When You Want More Than Just Sadhya

Here is the reality of modern Kerala weddings. Your guest list is diverse. You have got family members who grew up eating Sadhya every Onam, and you have got friends who would rather have biryani and kebabs. You might have colleagues from other states who have never seen a banana leaf meal in their lives.

A smart wedding menu acknowledges all of this.

### Live Counters

Live counters have become one of the most popular additions to Kerala wedding receptions, and for good reason. There is something exciting about watching food being made fresh right in front of you.

Options that work really well:

- **Dosa counter:** Mini dosas with an assortment of chutneys. Always draws a crowd.
- **Chaat counter:** Pani puri, bhel puri, dahi puri. Great as a starter while guests mingle.
- **Chinese counter:** Hakka noodles, fried rice, manchurian. It sounds out of place at a Kerala wedding, but honestly, this counter is always one of the busiest.
- **Pasta or Italian counter:** Increasingly popular at urban Kochi weddings.
- **Juice and smoothie bar:** Fresh tender coconut water, fruit smoothies, and mocktails.

Each live counter typically needs one to two dedicated staff members and a compact setup of about 6 to 8 feet of table space.

### Themed Additions

Some couples go further and add themed food sections:

- **Arabian corner:** Shawarma, hummus, grilled meats. Very popular in Kochi and Kozhikode.
- **Kerala street food section:** Pazhampori (banana fritters), sukhiyan, unniyappam, and egg puffs.
- **Dessert station:** Beyond payasam, think kulfi, ice cream, brownie bites, and fresh fruit platters.

The key with multi cuisine setups is not to overdo it. Three to four additional counters alongside the main Sadhya is the sweet spot. More than that and you start spreading your budget thin, and the quality of individual dishes can suffer.

We have seen weddings with 15 different food stations where nothing was particularly memorable. And we have seen weddings with a stunning Sadhya and two well executed live counters where guests could not stop raving. Focused and excellent beats scattered and average, every single time.

## Budget Planning and Guest Management

Let us talk money. Because no matter how beautiful your menu looks on paper, it needs to fit your budget.

### Cost Ranges for Kerala Wedding Catering

Here is a realistic breakdown of what wedding catering costs in Kochi and surrounding areas as of 2024 to 2025:

| Menu Type | Cost Per Plate (Approximate) |
|-----------|------------------------------|
| Basic Sadhya (20 to 22 dishes) | Rs. 450 to 650 |
| Full Sadhya (26 to 28 dishes + payasam) | Rs. 700 to 950 |
| Sadhya + Non Veg sides | Rs. 900 to 1,200 |
| Multi cuisine buffet (veg + non veg) | Rs. 800 to 1,500 |
| Premium with live counters | Rs. 1,200 to 2,000+ |

These numbers vary based on the number of dishes, ingredient quality, venue location, and the number of guests. Urban Kochi venues tend to cost slightly more than events in Aluva or Perumbavoor because of logistics.

### The Buffer Rule

Always plan for more food than your exact headcount. Always.

Our recommendation: prepare for 1.5 times your confirmed guest count, or at minimum add 15 to 20 percent on top.

Why? Because:

- Last minute additions are guaranteed. Relatives you did not expect, friends of friends, neighbors. It happens at every Kerala wedding.
- Some dishes get consumed faster than planned. Biryani and payasam always go quicker than you think.
- Running out of food at a wedding is the one thing you absolutely cannot afford. A little leftover is infinitely better than a guest finding an empty serving vessel.

### Smart Budget Tips

A few ways to keep costs reasonable without compromising on quality:

**Choose seasonal vegetables.** If your wedding is during monsoon, ash gourd and drumstick are abundant and cheap. Build your Sadhya around what is fresh and available.

**Limit non veg varieties.** Instead of five different non veg dishes, pick two or three and do them really well. A perfectly executed fish curry and chicken roast will impress more than five mediocre options.

**Opt for fewer live counters but do them right.** One incredible dosa counter is better than three half hearted stations.

**Discuss portion control with your caterer.** Experienced caterers know exactly how much of each dish to prepare. Trust their numbers rather than guessing yourself.

**Book early.** During peak wedding season in Kerala (November to February), caterers are in high demand. Booking three to four months ahead often gets you better rates and more attention.

## Designing Your Menu: A Step by Step Approach

If you are feeling overwhelmed, here is a simple framework we walk our clients through:

**Step 1: Decide your format.** Pure Sadhya? Sadhya plus non veg? Multi cuisine buffet? This single decision shapes everything else.

**Step 2: Set your budget per plate.** Be realistic. Share this number with your caterer upfront so they can design within your range.

**Step 3: Estimate your guest count.** Then add the buffer. If you expect 300, plan food for 350 to 360.

**Step 4: Choose your hero dishes.** Every wedding menu should have two or three standout items that people will remember. Maybe it is a special payasam recipe, or a prawn dish your family is known for, or a live counter that surprises guests.

**Step 5: Do a tasting.** This is non negotiable for weddings. Visit your caterer, taste the key dishes, and give feedback. A tasting session is where you move from a list on paper to confidence in your choice.

**Step 6: Finalize and confirm.** Lock in the menu at least two to three weeks before the wedding. This gives your caterer time to source ingredients and plan logistics without last minute scrambling.
